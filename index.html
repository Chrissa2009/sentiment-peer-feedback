Abstract

Graduate-level CS programs in the U.S. increasingly enroll international students, many of whom take online courses conducted in English. In such environments, peer feedback is a critical tool for engagement and learning. This paper investigates how native versus non-native English speaker status affects peer review sentiment, using the Twitter-roBERTa model to analyze 41,000 comments from 500 students. Results show that native speakers rate feedback less favorably, while non-native speakers write more positively but receive slightly less positive sentiment in return.

Research Questions

  • How do native and non-native English speakers perceive the impact of peer feedback?
  • How does sentiment differ in the feedback they write?
  • How does sentiment differ in the feedback they receive?

Methods

  • Dataset: Peer feedback from 500 students in Georgia Tech Online Master of Science in Computer Science (OMSCS) Program (2018â€“2022)
  • Model: Twitter-roBERTa-base
  • Analysis: Sentiment scoring of written and received feedback (range -1 to +1)

Key Findings

  • Non-native speakers write more positive feedback than native speakers
  • Native speakers rate the peer feedback system more negatively
  • Non-native speakers receive slightly less positive sentiment
  • Gender and age influenced both perception and expression of feedback
